Nostrana

Legendary chef Cathy Whims still kills at her sophisticated Buckman Italian restaurant, with its pastas, scissor-cut pizzas, and knockout Thursday-night gnocchi. Nostrana wows by honoring exceptional local ingredients with simplicity: Oregon-grown radicchio shines in insalata Nostrana, a Caesar with a cult following, while dishes like pasta and meatballs in three-ingredient tomato sauce (butter, tomato, and onion) are hard to forget.

-Eater PDX

Apizza Scholls

Hawthorne’s pizza cafe stills emerges as the town's best pie. Co-owner Brian Spangler shows respect for his dough, refusing to overload his crisp, quick-fired crusts with more toppings than they can handle. Thankfully, there's no need for DIY concoctions given the pizzeria's meticulous equation of sauce, fresh mozzarella, pecorino romano, and, in the case of the Pizza Amore, salty capicola scattered on top.

-Eater PDX

Ava Gene's

Executive chef Joshua McFadden workw closely with local farmers to create his dishes — especially toothsome pastas and expertly executed vegetables. The marble-topped bar is a nice place to wait for a table or grab a spot on a busy night, as is the bustling chef’s counter.

-Eater PDX

Hat Yai

Hat Yai’s shallot-fried chicken, salty and fragrant and just a touch sticky, pairs beautifully with Malayu-style curry and crispy roti, all available in the popular combo No. 1. The counter-service restaurant also offers cocktails, like a tamarind whiskey smash, easy to wash down with the wincingly spicy southern Thai ground pork salad over the restaurant’s blue-and-white checkered tablecloths.

-Eater PDX

Afuri

This Japanese ramen outpost chose Portland for its first international location because of the city’s water quality, key to broths like the highly delicate yuzu shio, a fragrant, citrusy base grounded in seafood, or the coat-the-spoon thick tonkotsu tantanmen with spicy sesame miso tare. In a bright, sunny space chef de cuisine Yoji Harada and executive chef Grant Macdonald incorporate Oregon clams and its beloved hazelnuts into bowls of thoroughly Japanese ramen.

-Eater PDX

Duck House

Duck House has finally provided Portland with a dreamy Chinese restaurant in the heart of the city. Since his 2016 arrival, San Diego chef Ivan Liu has played with heat and aromatics like a master, from delicate wontons with savory chile oil-spiked broth to tingly dan dan noodles with borderline-bitter bite. Deeply flavorful xiao long bao look their best in a table by the window, with a view of the game from mounted TVs.

-Eater PDX

Matt's BBQ

Portland isn’t exactly a barbecue destination, but pitmaster Matt Vicedomini changed the game with his Texas-style food cart, which almost always attracts lines from its corner of Prost Marketplace. The payoff is down-home decadent: fall-apart brisket with black-and-peppery bark, ribs that slip off the bone like a silk glove, and gooey queso macaroni and cheese.

-Eater PDX

Lardo

In the vein of Portland sandwich institutions like Bunk and Meat Cheese Bread, Lardo elevates what can be accomplished between two slices of bread, resulting in dripping, flavor-packed pork meatball banh mi and rotating "Chefwich" collaborations with top Portland chefs. The original Southeast location is still a favorite on nice days, thanks to the giant patio.

-Eater PDX

Jacqueline

Considering the abundance of wild, exquisite fish found in the Pacific Northwest, knockout seafood restaurants should be plentiful in Portland; still, Jacqueline on SE Clinton feels like the standout for creative, accessibly priced seafood. During happy hour, dollar oysters are fresh and briny and great with white wine; during dinner, a bright yellowtail crudo is just as delightful as the Oregonian-must-order Dungeness crab toast.

-Eater PDX

Ataula

Chef of the Year Jose Chesa emphasizes a convivial vibe at his modern Spanish gastropub in Northwest Portland. With a lively dining room facing an open kitchen, it's not a stuffy "tapas restaurant." The tapas themselves combine similar levels of comforting familiarity and playfulness: Beef and potato "bombas" arrive in a suitably spicy sauce; chorizo "lollipops" are addictive; and an almost-savory sangria is the ideal drink for rainy evenings.

-Eater PDX

Tusk

Boasting exceedingly close relationships with Oregon farmers, the Kerns restaurant is well-placed to succeed, and succeed it does. Executive chef and co-owner Sam Smith puts his experience at Philadelphia’s Zahav to work, delicately infusing Middle Eastern flavors with flair on both dinner and brunch menus, which center around grains, grilled meats, za’atar, haloumi, and all that produce.

-Eater PDX

Broder

A nod to the Pacific Northwest’s significant Nordic population, the cheerful Oregon chainlet of Scandinavian restaurants still attracts daunting lines for weekend breakfasts of spherical, powdered-sugar-covered aebleskiver (Danish pancakes), hammy cream-poached “lost eggs,” and elaborately adorned Broder boards. The Northern location is particularly ritzy, with white subway-tile bar and bright yellow touches, but nothing beats the original.

-Eater PDX
